Getting a diagnosis like this while running your own business brings a particular kind of uncertainty — not just about your health, but about everything you've built. Here is what to sort out, in order.

---

## Right now — this week

- **Pause major decisions.** You do not have to restructure your business, tell your clients, or change anything this week. Give yourself a few days before acting.
- **Call your GP or specialist's secretary.** Get a follow-up appointment confirmed and ask them to write down the diagnosis and what the next 30 days look like medically. You will need documentation in writing for insurance claims and any government applications.
- **Tell one trusted person.** Not your clients, not your accountant yet — just one person who can help you think clearly.
- **Locate your insurance documents.** This week, not next month. Details below on why this matters.

---

## What not to do yet

- **Do not wind down or dissolve your business.** As long as you are operating, you preserve options — contracts, income, and insurance eligibility. Closing the business prematurely can forfeit coverage you are entitled to.
- **Do not cancel any insurance policies.** Income protection, critical illness, life cover, mortgage protection — any of these may pay out on the basis of an MS diagnosis. Cancelling now could mean losing a payment you are already entitled to claim.
- **Do not agree to informal arrangements with clients verbally.** If you need to reduce your hours, defer a contract, or pause work, get any changes in writing. Verbal agreements about reduced scope or fees are hard to undo.
- **Do not make large irreversible financial moves** — selling assets, taking on new debt, restructuring property ownership — in the first few weeks. You do not yet have enough information about your financial picture to act wisely.
- **Do not stop contributing to your RRSP** if you are doing so. Some financial products attached to retirement savings carry insurance components — check before you change anything.
- **Do not assume you have no insurance cover.** Many self-employed people took out policies years ago and have forgotten the details. Check everything before assuming you are unprotected.

---

## Your employment rights

As a self-employed person, employment standards legislation does not apply to you in the same way it does to employees — you have no employer to request sick leave from and no statutory sick pay to claim. However, several things still matter:

- **Review your contracts now.** Look at force majeure, illness clauses, or termination provisions. If a contract requires you to deliver personally, understand what happens if you cannot — and whether there is a notice or renegotiation process.
- **You are not obliged to disclose your diagnosis to clients.** You can say you are dealing with a health matter without naming it. Your medical information is yours.
- **Disability as a protected ground.** Under Canadian human rights law — your provincial Human Rights Code, or the Canadian Human Rights Act if you work with federally regulated entities — disability is a protected ground. This is more relevant if you hold contracts with large organisations or government bodies that have obligations not to discriminate. If a client terminates a contract after learning of your diagnosis, that may be worth exploring with a human rights lawyer.
- **Consider whether you need to notify your insurer of any changes to your work pattern** before you actually change anything — some income protection policies have conditions around this.

## Your income

This is the section that matters most for self-employed people in Canada.

**Government support — what is and is not available to you:**

- **EI sickness benefits** are paid through Service Canada, but here is the critical point: self-employed people can only access EI special benefits — including sickness benefits — if they *registered* for the self-employed EI program in advance and have been contributing for at least 12 months. If you did not register, you cannot access EI sickness benefits now. Check your Service Canada account or call Service Canada to confirm whether you registered.
- **Canada Pension Plan disability benefit (CPP-D)** is a federal monthly payment for people with a severe and prolonged disability who have made sufficient CPP contributions. Whether MS qualifies as "severe and prolonged" for your specific situation is something your doctor needs to assess and document — this is not a quick payment, and there is an application and medical review process. But you should find out now whether you have enough CPP contributions to be eligible, and begin the process if appropriate. If you are in Quebec, this is the QPP through Retraite Québec.
- **The Canada Disability Benefit** is a federal payment for working-age people with disabilities — check current eligibility criteria with Service Canada, as this program is still being implemented and details are evolving.
- **Provincial disability income.** Each province has its own program — for example ODSP in Ontario, AISH in Alberta, or PWD assistance in British Columbia. These are means-tested and have their own eligibility rules. Search for your province's disability income assistance program or call 211 to find out what is available where you live.

**Insurance you may already hold — this is critical:**

Many self-employed people carry policies they took out when they set up their business — or years before — and have lost track of. Before you do anything else, search for:

- **Income protection / disability insurance** — a policy that replaces a percentage of your income if you cannot work. Check the stand-down period (how long before payments start), the benefit period, and whether partial incapacity is covered.
- **Critical illness or trauma insurance** — these frequently pay a lump sum on diagnosis of a listed condition, regardless of whether you can still work. MS is commonly listed. If you hold one of these, the payout may already be triggerable.
- **Life insurance** — some policies include a terminal illness advance or a serious illness rider. Check the wording.
- **Mortgage protection or loan protection insurance** — covers repayments if you are unable to work. Many people hold these without fully remembering the terms.
- **Business overhead insurance** — a policy specifically for self-employed people that covers business running costs while you are unable to work. Check if you have one.

To find out what you hold: contact every insurer or broker you have ever dealt with and ask them directly — "What cover do I have under this policy, and what are the claim conditions?" Get the answer in writing. Do not assume you do not qualify before you have checked the actual policy wording. This step alone could change your financial picture significantly.

## Your insurance

Standalone policies are separate from anything government-related — and for a self-employed person, they are often the main safety net.

- **Income protection** — check the definition of disability in your policy. Some policies pay out when you cannot perform your *own occupation*; others use a stricter definition. Understand which yours uses.
- **Critical illness / trauma cover** — as noted above, many of these pay on diagnosis of a listed condition. MS is frequently listed. The payout is typically a lump sum, separate from any income replacement.
- **Total and permanent disability (TPD) cover** — this is usually a higher bar, but worth checking the definition in your policy.
- **Group benefits through a professional association** — some contractors and self-employed people hold coverage through industry associations, professional bodies, or chambers of commerce. Check whether you belong to any organisation that provides group benefits.

Find your policy documents — or call the insurer or broker — and confirm in writing what you hold and the claim conditions. Do not cancel or alter any policy until you have done this.

## Your treatment journey

- **You have the right to a second opinion.** Ask your neurologist or GP directly: "Can you refer me for a second opinion?" In most provinces this is straightforward. You do not need to be apologetic about asking.
- **At your next appointment, bring someone with you** — even on the phone. They can take notes while you are listening.
- **Ask your specialist to write down:** the diagnosis, what the next 30 days look like, what treatments are being considered, and who the key contacts are.
- **Ask for copies of all test results and clinical notes.** You are entitled to these. They will matter for insurance claims and any government benefit applications.
- **Your provincial health plan** covers your specialist and hospital care under the Canada Health Act. If there are costs not covered — certain medications, therapies, or equipment — ask the MS clinic or your neurologist what assistance programs exist. MS Canada can also point you toward drug access programs.
- **MS clinics** exist in most major Canadian cities and offer multidisciplinary care. Ask for a referral if you are not already connected to one.
- **Provincial patient ombudsman.** If you have concerns about your care, most provinces have a Patient Ombudsman or equivalent — search for your province's office.

## Practical and legal

This is housekeeping, not catastrophising. Getting these in order is sensible for any business owner.

- **Make or update your will.** If your business assets need to be dealt with, a will that does not account for them creates problems.
- **Set up a Power of Attorney — two of them.** One for personal care (who makes healthcare decisions if you cannot) and one for property and finances (who manages your money and business if you cannot). These are separate documents. A notary or lawyer can prepare them. Do it while you are well and clear-headed.
- **Advance care directive.** A written statement of what care you would or would not want. Ask your GP or the MS clinic how to document this.
- **Update beneficiaries** on your RRSP, any life insurance, and any other registered accounts. These pass outside your will — a named beneficiary overrides everything else.
- **Document your business processes.** Not because you are going anywhere, but because if you need someone to step in temporarily, clear documentation makes that possible without chaos.
- **Store these documents somewhere your trusted person can find them** — and tell them where that is.

## The people in your life

Disclosure is yours to control. You never have to name the diagnosis to anyone — not clients, not family, not colleagues — until you are ready, and not at all if you choose not to.

A useful order: tell one or two people who will be calm and practical first. Then wider family and friends when you feel ready. There is no obligation to tell everyone, or to tell anyone on any particular timeline.

You can ask one trusted person to pass news on to others so you do not have to repeat the same conversation. Set the terms — whether you want help, information, or just for people to know.

Some people will react badly, say the wrong thing, or make it about themselves. You are allowed to limit time with anyone who makes this harder.

## Support

**MS Canada** is the main national organisation for people living with MS in Canada. They provide information about the condition, connections to MS clinics, financial assistance programs for medications and equipment, peer support, and an MS Navigator service — trained professionals who can help you find local resources. Visit mscanada.ca or call 1-800-268-7582.

**211** is a free service that connects you to local community, social, and government services in your province. Call 2-1-1 or visit 211.ca. For a self-employed person trying to work out what provincial disability programs and supports exist in your area, this is a fast way to get pointed in the right direction.

**A financial adviser with disability insurance experience** — not a general investment adviser, but someone who understands disability and income protection claims — can be genuinely useful at this stage. Ask MS Canada or your broker for a referral.
