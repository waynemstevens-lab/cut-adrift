Getting a serious diagnosis while you're not working brings a particular kind of pressure — financial uncertainty on top of everything else. Here is what to sort through first.

---

## Right now — this week

- **Pause big decisions.** You do not need to figure everything out this week. Give yourself a few days before taking any irreversible steps.
- **Tell one trusted person.** Not everyone — one person who can sit with you and help you think clearly.
- **Confirm your next medical appointment and ask for a written summary.** Ask the specialist's secretary to confirm your next steps in writing, including the diagnosis and the proposed plan. You will need this for almost everything else in this plan.
- **Gather your financial paperwork.** Start pulling together any insurance policies, pension or group benefits documents, and records of your PRSI — wait, strike that. In Canada, gather records of your CPP contributions, any private insurance policies, and any employer group benefits documents you may still have from previous jobs.

---

## What not to do yet

- **Do not cancel any insurance policies** — life cover, critical illness, income protection, mortgage protection. Many policies pay out on diagnosis itself, not on inability to work. Cancelling now could forfeit a payout you are entitled to right now.
- **Do not make irreversible financial moves** — selling assets, cashing out investments, taking on new debt, or withdrawing from an RRSP — in the first few weeks. The full picture of what you are entitled to is not clear yet.
- **Do not assume you don't qualify for benefits** because you are not currently working. Several Canadian federal and provincial programs are available regardless of recent employment history.
- **Do not update or alter any insurance beneficiaries in haste.** Wait until you have a clear picture of what you hold and what it pays.
- **Do not ignore previous employer benefit plans.** If you were employed in the past few years, you may still have group insurance or coverage active — it is worth checking.

---

## Your employment rights

Because you are not currently working, detailed employment-rights guidance does not apply to your situation right now. If you return to work in the future, your provincial Human Rights Code and the duty on employers to accommodate disabilities will be relevant — disability is a protected ground in every province, and federally regulated employers are bound by the Canadian Human Rights Act. Keep that in your back pocket.

---

## Your income

Since you are not currently employed, you will not qualify for Employment Insurance (EI) sickness benefits — those require recent insurable employment. But several other programs may apply.

**Federal programs — check with Service Canada:**

- **Canada Pension Plan Disability Benefit (CPP-D):** If you have made enough CPP contributions in your working life, you may qualify for CPP-D, which supports people with a severe and prolonged disability. Call Service Canada or visit their website to check your contribution history and eligibility. If you live in Quebec, the equivalent is the Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) disability benefit through Retraite Québec.
- **Canada Disability Benefit:** A federal payment for working-age people with disabilities. Check current eligibility criteria with Service Canada — the program has been developing, so confirm what is available now.

**Provincial programs — these vary and may be more immediately accessible:**

- In **Ontario**: Ontario Disability Support Program (ODSP)
- In **Alberta**: Assured Income for the Severely Handicapped (AISH)
- In **British Columbia**: Persons with Disabilities (PWD) assistance

If you are in another province or territory, search for "[your province] disability income assistance." Call 2-1-1 or visit 211.ca — they can point you to the specific program in your area and help you understand how to apply.

**Healthcare costs:**

Your provincial or territorial health plan (for example OHIP in Ontario, RAMQ in Quebec, MSP in British Columbia) covers most medically necessary care. But a chronic illness often brings drug costs, specialist fees, and equipment costs that fall outside the public plan. Ask your doctor or specialist's office about:

- Your province's drug benefit program — many provinces have catastrophic drug coverage or a drug plan for people on disability income.
- Whether you qualify for a provincial pharmacare or supplementary benefits program.

**Hidden insurance you may have and may not realise:**

This is one of the most important things in this plan. People often have cover they have forgotten about or never properly checked.

- **Previous employer group benefits** — if you had a job in the past, check whether you held group life cover, critical illness, or income protection through that employer. Sometimes coverage continues or a claim window exists.
- **Mortgage protection / loan protection insurance** — if you have a mortgage, loan, or even some credit cards, there may be an attached insurance policy that pays out on diagnosis of a serious illness.
- **Trauma or critical illness cover** — a policy you may have taken out years ago, possibly through a broker. These frequently pay a lump sum on diagnosis of a listed condition, regardless of whether you can work.
- **RRSP or group plan insurance** — some RRSP products and group savings plans include insurance components.

To find out: contact each provider or insurer directly and ask: *"What cover do I have under this membership or policy, and what are the claim conditions?"* Get the answer in writing. Do this before making any changes to any policy.

## Your insurance

Even though you are not currently working, standalone insurance policies you hold privately are completely separate from employment — and worth checking carefully.

- **Critical illness / trauma cover** — pays a lump sum on diagnosis of a listed condition. Many people are surprised to find they qualify for a payout without being unable to work.
- **Income protection** — if you hold a policy, check the stand-down period and the definition of disability it uses. Some policies apply even if you are not currently employed.
- **Life cover** — some life policies include a serious illness advance or a terminal illness benefit. Check the wording.
- **Total and permanent disability (TPD)** — if you hold this cover, check whether your condition meets the definition.

Find your policy documents or call the insurer or your broker and ask what you hold and what the claim conditions are. Many of these policies pay out on diagnosis itself — not on inability to work — so do not assume you don't qualify before checking the wording. Do not cancel or alter anything until you have confirmed this in writing.

## Your treatment journey

- **You have the right to a second opinion.** In Canada, you can ask your GP or specialist to refer you to another specialist. You do not need a reason. Say: *"I would like a referral for a second opinion."*
- **Bring someone to your next appointment** — a friend or family member to take notes. Ask the specialist to write down the diagnosis, the proposed treatment plan, and what the next 30 days look like.
- **Ask for copies of all test results, imaging, and clinical letters.** You are entitled to your own health records. Ask the clinic how to request them.
- **Know your patient rights.** Canada does not have a single national patient rights charter — these are set provincially. Most provinces have a Patient Ombudsman or equivalent body. Search for "[your province] patient ombudsman" or "[your province] patient rights" to find the body that covers you.
- **Public and private care:** Your provincial plan covers most medically necessary care, but wait times for specialists can be long. If you have private health insurance (current or from a previous employer), check whether it covers faster access, private specialists, or extended services.

## Practical and legal

This is housekeeping — it is about being prepared, not about expecting the worst.

- **Make or update your will.** If you do not have one, now is a good time. A notary (in Quebec) or a lawyer (in other provinces) can draw one up.
- **Set up Powers of Attorney.** In most Canadian provinces these are two separate documents: one for personal care / healthcare decisions, and one for property / finances. These allow a person you trust to make decisions on your behalf if you cannot. The names vary by province — search for "[your province] power of attorney personal care" and "[your province] power of attorney property."
- **Make an advance care directive.** Also called a living will in some provinces — this sets out what care you would and would not want. Ask your GP or specialist's office for guidance on the form used in your province.
- **Update beneficiaries.** Check and update the beneficiaries named on your RRSP, any life insurance, and any pension. These pass outside your will, so a named beneficiary overrides it.
- **Store documents somewhere accessible.** Tell the person you trust where to find your will, powers of attorney, insurance policies, and advance directive.

## The people in your life

Who you tell, when, and how much — that is entirely yours to control.

- Disclosure is one-way. Once someone knows, they know. You can choose to share a lot, a little, or nothing specific — you never have to name the diagnosis to anyone.
- A gentle order helps: one or two trusted people first, then wider family and friends when you feel ready. There is no deadline and no obligation to tell everyone.
- You can ask one trusted person to pass the news to others, so you do not have to repeat yourself. Set the terms — tell them what you want people to know, and whether you want help, space, or just for people to be aware.
- Some people will react badly or make it about themselves. That is their limitation, not yours. You are allowed to create distance from anyone who makes this harder.

## Support

**Condition-specific support:**
You haven't named your diagnosis, and you don't have to — but if and when you want it, there are condition-specific charities and organisations in Canada for most serious chronic illnesses (for example MS Canada, Parkinson Canada, the Heart & Stroke Foundation, Diabetes Canada, and many others). Search for "[your condition] Canada" to find the national organisation, and check whether they have a provincial chapter near you. These organisations often have navigators, helplines, and peer support programmes.

**General support:**

- **211 Canada** — call 2-1-1 or visit 211.ca. Free, confidential, available in most of Canada. They connect you to local health, social, and financial services including disability support programs in your area. This is the single best starting point for finding what is available where you live.
- **Canadian Cancer Society** — if your diagnosis is cancer, their helpline (1 888 939 3333) offers information, emotional support, and practical navigation, including benefits guidance.
- **Your provincial health authority** — many provinces have patient navigators or social workers attached to specialist clinics. Ask your specialist's office whether one is available to you. They can help coordinate care and connect you to financial support.
